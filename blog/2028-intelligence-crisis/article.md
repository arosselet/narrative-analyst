# The Debt That Cannot See Deflation

*By Narrative Analyst | March 3, 2026*

![The friction economy being crushed — and price tags dropping to near-zero](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arosselet/narrative-analyst/main/blog/assets/friction_economy_collapse.png)

By early 2026, Wall Street was producing a specific genre of research note: the scenario document. Dressed in the language of intellectual humility — "thought exercise," "not a prediction," "tail-risk modeling" — these pieces sketch the plausible mechanics of a financial cataclysm triggered by artificial intelligence. The most sophisticated version of this genre traces a mechanism that is genuinely worth taking seriously: AI agents replacing not just human workers but the software those workers used, unwinding the debt architecture built on the assumption that software subscriptions were permanent.

The mechanism is real. The model built around it contains a structural blind spot so large that it determines the conclusions before the analysis begins.

## The Financial Architecture That Was Always a Fiction

For the better part of a decade, private equity firms executed a strategy that sounds baroque until you realize it was entirely legal and heavily incentivized. They acquired life insurance companies — businesses with access to stable, regulated streams of annuity deposits from the American middle class — and used that capital as the base for leveraged buyouts of SaaS companies. The pitch to regulators was that software subscription revenue was as stable as utility income. The pitch to the firms themselves was that they were earning venture-scale returns on bond-rated collateral.

The hidden assumption sustaining the whole structure was that the software being acquired was genuinely useful — that people would keep paying for it because it solved a real problem that no alternative addressed more cheaply.

AI agents are the alternative.

When agentic systems can perform the work for which enterprise software was previously licensed, the "annual recurring revenue" that underwrote the debt stops recurring. The software becomes optional. The LBO collateral turns out not to have been AAA-rated utility income; it was a fee extracted from organizational inertia, now resolved.

The scenario analyses are right about this. Where they go wrong is in how they model the consequence.

## The Blind Spot Built Into the Lens

The authors of these scenario documents are, professionally, capital allocators. They hold assets — loans, bonds, structured credit — denominated in fixed future payments. The thing that kills a fixed-future-payment instrument is deflation: a world where the purchasing power of money rises because goods and services get cheaper.

When the friction economy collapses, the most direct consequence is that services which currently cost significant money become nearly free or dramatically cheaper: legal research, accounting, tax preparation, customer support, software development, real estate transaction processing, logistics routing, medical coding. The list of "friction" that AI agents eliminate is almost exactly the list of services that presently constitute large line items in both corporate expense budgets and middle-class household spending.

The scenario narratives model the income side of this collapse with precision — jobs lost, wages compressed, spending power destroyed. They consistently omit the liability side: the same technological force that destroys wages also destroys the cost of living. A family that loses $40,000 in annual income but also saves $30,000 in previously unavoidable service fees and subscription costs is in a categorically different financial position than a family that simply loses $40,000.

This omission is not a mistake. It is a feature of the lens. The fixed-rate paper that institutional debt holders carry does not become more manageable when consumer prices fall. It becomes more burdensome — the real value of the debt obligation rises relative to a deflating cost of living. Deflation is good for consumers and catastrophic for bondholders. The scenario documents, written by and for bondholders, find it structurally impossible to model deflation as a partially beneficial outcome.

## The State as a Paralyzed Spectator

The standard 2028-crisis scenario also treats the government as effectively impotent: income tax revenues collapse, social safety net costs spike, the Federal Reserve lacks the tools to address a structural deflation, and intervention becomes politically incoherent.

This analysis would be more credible if the last decade of evidence suggested that sovereign states, when facing genuinely existential fiscal crises, tend toward paralysis rather than extraordinary authority. They do not. What governments lose in income tax revenue in an AI-disrupted economy, they gain in the ability to assert new claims on the infrastructure of intelligence production: compute taxation, mandatory revenue sharing, nationalization of training infrastructure, algorithmic redistribution mechanisms.

None of these are politically easy. All of them are structurally available to a sovereign state that has concluded — correctly — that the alternative is the dissolution of its tax base.

## What to Watch For

The 2028 crisis scenario is most useful not as a prediction but as a map of which interests are structurally invisible in institutional financial modeling. The measurable test of whether the blind spot is being corrected: watch for the first major macro-finance research note that explicitly models the deflationary consumption dividend alongside the wage-compression scenario and concludes that the net consumer welfare effect is positive even in a significant-disruption case.

If that note appears, it will mean the analytical framing has finally escaped the perspective of fixed-rate paper holders and begun accounting for the humans on the other side of the economy. If it does not appear by 2028, the genre will have succeeded in its actual function: convincing the policy environment that the coming disruption is a crisis requiring urgent intervention on behalf of the financial system, rather than a difficult transition with mixed distributional effects that require a substantially different set of responses.

The debt cannot see the deflation. The question is whether the policymakers can.
