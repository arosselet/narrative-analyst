# Worked Example: Anthropic, the Pentagon, and the AI Safety Paradox

*February 2026*

This example demonstrates the Actor-Narrative Framework applied to a controversy unfolding right now: Anthropic's refusal to grant the Pentagon unrestricted military use of its Claude AI model, the Trump administration's retaliatory designation of the company as a "supply chain risk to national security," and the simultaneous loosening of Anthropic's own safety commitments under competitive pressure.

This is the protocol applied, not described. What follows is what the framework actually produces.

---

## Source Material

The analysis draws on reporting from PBS, The Washington Post, The Guardian, CBS News, Breaking Defense, and Forbes, as well as Anthropic's own published Responsible Scaling Policy 3.0, Dario Amodei's public statements, and Pentagon press briefings from February 2026.

---

## Step 1: Research and Augment Knowledge

Anthropic was founded in 2021 by former OpenAI employees, including siblings Dario and Daniela Amodei, who left over disagreements about safety practices. The company positioned itself as the "safety-first" AI lab, an identity central to its fundraising narrative and public brand. Its Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP), introduced in 2023, was the industry's most concrete commitment: if a model reached certain dangerous capability thresholds, Anthropic would halt development until safety measures caught up. No exceptions.

By early 2026, Claude was the only frontier AI model operating on classified Pentagon networks. It was used for intelligence analysis, modeling and simulation, and operational planning. Reports indicate Claude was involved in military applications including the U.S. capture of Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro in early 2026. This relationship was quietly lucrative: approximately $200 million in contracts.

Then two things happened in rapid succession:

**February 24-26, 2026**: Anthropic published RSP 3.0, removing its "hard pause" commitment. Under the new policy, Anthropic would only halt development if it had a "significant lead" over competitors or if competitors also had robust safety measures. If competitors were advancing with weaker safeguards, Anthropic stated it "will not necessarily delay AI development and deployment."

**Late February 2026**: The Pentagon demanded unrestricted military use of Claude for "all lawful purposes." Anthropic refused, drawing what CEO Dario Amodei called a "sharp red line" against mass surveillance of Americans and fully autonomous weapons. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security," a classification normally reserved for foreign adversaries. President Trump called the company "Leftwing nut jobs." All federal agencies were ordered to cease using Anthropic's technology.

These two events, occurring within days of each other, create the analytical tension that a standard summary would gloss over.

---

## Step 2: Faction Mapping and Lexicon Deconstruction

### Faction: Anthropic (The Safety-Branded Lab)

**Worldview**: AI is powerful enough to be dangerous, and the company building it has a moral obligation to set limits on its use. Some applications cross ethical lines that no amount of money or government pressure should move. However, safety principles must be adapted to competitive realities. A lone responsible developer pausing while others press ahead could lead to a less safe world.

**Rhetorical strategy**: Anthropic's public messaging during the Pentagon dispute uses the language of moral clarity: "cannot in good conscience accede," "sharp red line," "mass surveillance of Americans." These are absolute terms designed to position the refusal as principled rather than negotiable.

**Language elevation**: "responsible scaling," "safety leadership," "ethical guardrails," "principled refusal"
**Language denigration**: The Pentagon's demands are framed as "unrestricted," implying recklessness.

**Critical lexicon shift (RSP 3.0)**: The earlier RSP used the word "commitment." RSP 3.0 replaces it with "aim" and "performance standards." This is the difference between a promise and a goal. The phrase "will not necessarily delay" is doing enormous work: it preserves the appearance of a policy while eliminating the obligation. "Not necessarily" is the rhetorical equivalent of a fire exit.

**Euphemisms**: RSP 3.0 describes removing the hard pause as a shift from "rigid hard stops" to a "regulatory ladder where safeguards scale proportionally with capability growth." This reframes the abandonment of a concrete safety mechanism as an increase in sophistication.

### Faction: The Trump Administration / Pentagon

**Worldview**: National security requires unfettered access to the most advanced tools available. AI companies operating on classified networks with taxpayer-funded contracts do not get to unilaterally decide what the military can and cannot do with licensed technology. A private company refusing a lawful government demand is insubordination, not principle.

**Rhetorical strategy**: Frame Anthropic's refusal as a threat to national security and to American lives. Use classification language ("supply chain risk") that associates a domestic AI company with foreign adversaries. Deploy the President's personal brand of rhetorical denigration ("Leftwing nut jobs") to make the issue cultural rather than technical.

**Language elevation**: "all lawful purposes," "national security," "American lives"
**Language denigration**: "Leftwing nut jobs," "strong-arming the Pentagon"

**Named tactic (False Dilemma)**: The choice is framed as binary: either grant unrestricted access or endanger national security. No middle ground is acknowledged. The possibility of use-restricted contracts, which are standard in defense procurement, is absent from the administration's public framing.

**Named tactic (Penalty as Precedent)**: The "supply chain risk" designation isn't just punitive toward Anthropic. It's a signal to every other AI company about the cost of refusal. Any company "contracting with the U.S. military" is now barred from commercial activity with Anthropic. This creates an industry-wide incentive structure: comply or be isolated.

### Faction: Competing AI Companies (OpenAI, xAI, Google)

**Worldview**: Largely invisible in public statements, but revealed through actions. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman publicly supported Anthropic's principled stance while OpenAI simultaneously maintained its own Pentagon contracts under the required terms. xAI's Grok is reportedly being integrated into military networks. Google dropped its earlier pledge not to use AI for weapons or surveillance.

**Rhetorical strategy**: Silence as strategy. By saying nothing while complying, competitors avoid both the moral exposure of publicly endorsing unrestricted military AI and the business exposure of publicly refusing it. Altman's statement of support costs nothing since OpenAI is already compliant, and positions him as sympathetic while his company benefits from Anthropic's exclusion.

**Named tactic (Conspicuous Absence)**: None of the compliant companies have issued public statements explaining what safeguards, if any, they insisted on in their own Pentagon agreements. The absence of this information is analytically significant: if they had strong safeguards, they would say so. Their silence suggests the terms they accepted are ones they don't want publicly associated with their brands.

### Faction: Departing Safety Researchers

**Worldview**: The safety commitments that attracted them to these companies were real, but the competitive and political pressures have made those commitments untenable. The gap between what these companies say about safety and what they do is growing.

**Key figures**: Mrinank Sharma, Anthropic's head of Safeguards Research, resigned citing concerns about the direction of safety policy. Former OpenAI researcher Zoë Hitzig published an essay comparing OpenAI to Facebook's trajectory of prioritizing engagement over ethics.

**Rhetorical strategy**: Personal testimony and public resignation as acts of dissent. These individuals convert institutional insider knowledge into public credibility.

**Conspicuous absence**: These voices are the most empirically informed participants in the debate, yet they receive a fraction of the coverage given to corporate executives and government officials.

## Step 3: Timeline to the Causal Schism

The critical sequence:

1. **2021**: Anthropic founded explicitly as the "safety-first" alternative to OpenAI.
2. **2023**: RSP published with hard pause commitment. Industry-leading safety stance becomes central to fundraising and brand identity.
3. **2024-2025**: Claude deployed on classified Pentagon networks. Approximately $200 million in contracts.
4. **Early 2026**: Reports surface that Claude was used in military operations including the Maduro capture.
5. **Feb 24-26, 2026**: RSP 3.0 published, removing the hard pause. Anthropic cites competitive pressure and the failure of voluntary industry alignment.
6. **Late Feb 2026**: Pentagon demands unrestricted use. Anthropic refuses. "Supply chain risk" designation follows.

**The causal schism** isn't the Pentagon dispute. It occurred earlier: the moment Anthropic's identity as a safety-first company collided with the economic reality that its largest government customer wanted to use its product in ways the company's principles prohibited. RSP 3.0 can be read as an attempt to resolve this tension by loosening safety commitments in one domain (competitive development) while holding firm in another (military applications). Whether this is principled pragmatism or strategic inconsistency is the central interpretive question.

---

## Step 4: Power Structure and Economic Incentives

**Anthropic's financial position**: The company was reportedly preparing for an IPO. The "supply chain risk" designation doesn't just cancel government contracts; it bars any company working with the Pentagon from doing business with Anthropic. This extends the damage from a single customer loss to potential commercial isolation. The economic pressure is designed to exceed the cost of compliance.

**The Pentagon's leverage**: Claude was "deeply embedded in military platforms" with a six-month phase-out period. This creates a window of vulnerability where the government is both punishing Anthropic and dependent on its technology. The leverage is temporal: Anthropic's bargaining position weakens as the phase-out clock runs down.

**Competitor incentives**: Every company that complied with the Pentagon's terms gains market share from Anthropic's exclusion. The structure creates a classic defection problem: the company that holds the line on safety principles bears the cost, while those that defect capture the reward.

**The safety-to-revenue pipeline**: Anthropic's safety brand is also a fundraising brand. The RSP was cited in investor materials. Investors who bought in on the safety thesis now watch the company simultaneously loosen its development safety policy (RSP 3.0) and refuse a lucrative government contract on safety grounds. These two actions are in tension, and the financial stakes make it impossible to analyze the safety claims without analyzing the business model.

---

## Step 5: Asymmetries and Marginalized Voices

**Who isn't at the table**: The public, which has no role in determining the terms under which AI is deployed for surveillance or autonomous weapons. Nor are the communities most likely to be affected by mass surveillance: the very populations Anthropic says it's protecting by refusing.

**Information asymmetry**: The specific military applications of Claude on classified networks are unknown to the public. The full terms of the Pentagon's demand are unknown. The full terms of competing companies' agreements are unknown. Nearly every consequential fact in this controversy is classified, proprietary, or both. Public analysis is necessarily operating on what the factions choose to reveal.

**Structural asymmetry**: The Pentagon can unilaterally designate companies as security risks. Companies cannot correspondingly challenge the designation through any rapid legal mechanism. The power differential is near-absolute.

---

## Step 6: External Corroboration

**Anthropic's RSP 3.0** is publicly available and verifiable. The specific language, "will not necessarily delay AI development and deployment," is a primary source, not a media interpretation.

**The "supply chain risk" designation** is a formal regulatory action with legal consequences, not a rhetorical flourish. Its previous applications have been to entities like Huawei and Kaspersky, foreign companies with alleged ties to adversarial governments. Its application to a domestic AI company is unprecedented.

**The departure of Mrinank Sharma** and his stated reasons are on the public record. His position as head of Safeguards Research at Anthropic gives his departure specific evidentiary weight that a generic employee resignation would not carry.

**Research directives for deeper investigation**:
- What were the specific military applications Claude was used for on classified networks?
- What terms did OpenAI, Google, and xAI accept in their Pentagon contracts? Are there use restrictions?
- What is the relationship between Anthropic's IPO timeline and the RSP 3.0 revision?
- How many safety researchers have departed Anthropic since RSP 3.0, and what are their stated reasons?

## Step 7: Competing Grand Narratives

**Anthropic's story**: "We built this company to prove that AI development and safety aren't contradictory. We put real commitments in writing when nobody else would. Yes, we adapted our development policy because unilateral disarmament doesn't make the world safer. But we drew the line where it matters most: we will not build tools for mass surveillance or autonomous killing. The Pentagon wants unconditional obedience. We offered partnership with principles. They chose punishment."

**The Pentagon's story**: "We license the most advanced AI in the world to protect Americans. We have protocols, we have oversight, and we operate within the law. A private company deciding which lawful orders it will and won't support is not a safety policy. It's insubordination with a press release. If Anthropic wants to sell us technology, they sell it under our terms, the same terms every defense contractor has accepted for decades. You don't get to be a conscience-for-hire."

**The competitor's story** (unspoken): "We said yes because that's what contractors do. We negotiated quietly, implemented practical safeguards where we could, and stayed in the room where decisions get made. Anthropic's grand gesture means they're locked out and we're the ones shaping how military AI actually gets deployed. Sometimes the pragmatic choice is the moral one."

**The departing researcher's story**: "I joined Anthropic because the RSP meant something. It was the first time a frontier lab put a concrete, falsifiable commitment on paper. Then the market reality hit, and the commitment became a 'performance standard.' Then the military contract raised the stakes, and they drew a line on surveillance while erasing the line on development speed. They're choosing which principles to keep based on which ones are publicly visible. I can't be part of performing safety while the substance erodes."

## What the Framework Reveals

This is where the decomposition pays off. A standard LLM analysis of this controversy would likely conclude something like: "Anthropic's principled refusal demonstrates the tensions between AI safety and national security, highlighting the need for clear regulatory frameworks." That's not wrong. It's also not useful. It's the narrative equivalent of reporting the weather.

The framework reveals:

**1. The RSP 3.0 timing creates a coherence problem that nobody is addressing.**

Anthropic loosened its development safety commitments and drew a hard line on military safety commitments *within the same week*. The standard media narrative treats these as separate stories: "Anthropic adapts to competition" and "Anthropic defies Pentagon." The framework's timeline analysis (Step 3) makes them one story. The question it surfaces: is Anthropic drawing a red line on military use because it genuinely believes that's the most critical safety boundary, or because military refusal is publicly visible (and brand-reinforcing) while development speed is not?

This is not a gotcha question. Both answers could be true simultaneously. But a useful analysis has to surface the question, and a standard synthesis will not.

**2. The "supply chain risk" designation is a narrative weapon with a specific audience.**

It's not primarily aimed at Anthropic. It's aimed at every other AI company considering whether to push back on government demands. The framework's power structure analysis (Step 4) identifies this: the designation creates a defection incentive. Companies that comply are rewarded with monopoly access. Companies that resist are economically isolated. This converts a bilateral dispute into an industry-wide enforcement mechanism. The narrative of "Anthropic was punished for being principled" is true, but incomplete. The strategic function is deterrence.

**3. Competitor silence is the most important data point in the controversy.**

OpenAI, Google, and xAI have all accepted Pentagon contracts without disclosing their terms. Sam Altman publicly supported Anthropic while his company quietly complied. The framework's conspicuous absence analysis (Step 2) flags this silence as analytically significant: If these companies had negotiated strong ethical guardrails, they would publicize them. They haven't. The absence of information is itself information. This suggests the "all lawful purposes" standard that Anthropic refused is the standard the others accepted.

**4. The departure of safety researchers is an underweighted leading indicator.**

Media coverage frames the Anthropic-Pentagon dispute as the main event. The framework's asymmetry analysis (Step 5) identifies the quiet exodus of safety researchers, including Anthropic's own head of Safeguards Research, as potentially more significant. These individuals have inside knowledge of how safety policies are actually implemented (versus how they're described in public documents). Their departures are individual data points that, in aggregate, constitute a pattern. The framework generates a specific research directive: map the departures across all frontier labs, their stated reasons, and the timing relative to policy changes.

**5. Every faction is performing for a different audience.**

Anthropic is performing for investors and the safety community (principled refusal). The Pentagon is performing for defense hawks and compliant contractors (enforcement of authority). Competitors are performing for the Pentagon (compliance) while not performing for the public (silence). Departing researchers are performing for the historical record (public testimony). An LLM trained on all of these performances will average them into a moderate synthesis. The framework's value is in revealing that the "moderate synthesis" is no one's actual position, it's a statistical artifact of competing performances.
